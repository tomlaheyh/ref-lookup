# 2026 ACC/AHA Dyslipidemia Guideline — Complete Review Notes (Pages 1–90)

**Document:** 2026 ACC/AHA/AACVPR/ABC/ACPM/ADA/AGS/APhA/ASPC/NLA/PCNA Guideline on the Management of Dyslipidemia
**DOI:** 10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423
**Published:** March 13, 2026 in Circulation
**Chair:** Roger S. Blumenthal, MD

**Context:** These notes were compiled across a multi-part review of the complete guideline (~90 clinical pages) in preparation for a US vs EU guideline comparison with the 2025 ESC/EAS Focused Update on Dyslipidaemias (DOI: 10.1093/eurheartj/ehaf190). The review covers all clinical sections (Sections 1–6). Only references remain unreviewed.

---

## Overview

This is a **complete replacement** of the 2018 AHA/ACC cholesterol guideline — not a focused update. It is retitled from "Management of Blood Cholesterol" to "Management of Dyslipidemia" to reflect broader scope covering LDL-C, triglycerides, Lp(a), and apoB. The document is comprehensive (~90 clinical pages plus references), heavily table- and flowchart-driven, and covers primary prevention, secondary prevention, and numerous special populations.

---

## Pages 1–10: Title, What Is New, Introduction, Definitions

### Key Structural Points
- Developed with 11 collaborating medical associations
- Literature search October 2024–December 2024, with select studies through April 2025
- Incorporates evidence from major post-2018 CVOTs: FOURIER, ODYSSEY OUTCOMES, CLEAR Outcomes, REDUCE-IT, and others

### Table 1 — "What Is New" (Pages 4–8)
A massive table spanning 5 pages listing all new and revised recommendations. Major changes include:

- **PREVENT-ASCVD equations** replace the old Pooled Cohort Equations (PCE) for risk assessment — the "CPR" model (Calculate, Personalize, Reclassify)
- **LDL-C and non-HDL-C treatment goals restored** (the 2018 guideline had moved away from specific targets)
- **ApoB measurement** now recommended for guiding therapy in patients with ASCVD, CKM syndrome, diabetes, or elevated TG
- **Lp(a) measurement** recommended at least once in all adults (COR 1)
- **CAC scoring** gets a massively expanded role with granular recommendations based on score ranges (0, 1–99, 100–299, 300–999, ≥1000)
- **Bempedoic acid and inclisiran** added to the treatment armamentarium
- **Secondary prevention goals**: LDL-C <55 mg/dL for very high risk, <70 mg/dL for not very high risk
- **New sections** on subclinical coronary atherosclerosis, HIV, cancer survivors, pregnancy, CKD, hypertriglyceridemia, and more

### COR/LOE System (Page 9)
Uses the standard ACC/AHA classification — Class 1 (Strong), 2a (Moderate), 2b (Weak), 3 (No Benefit or Harm), with Levels A through C-EO.

### Definitions (Page 10)
- CKM syndrome formally defined (obesity, insulin resistance, dyslipidemia, hypertension, CKD)
- Clinical ASCVD defined: ACS, MI, stable or unstable angina, revascularization, stroke, TIA, PAD
- Very high risk ASCVD: ≥2 major ASCVD events OR 1 major event + ≥2 high-risk conditions
- Scope: LDL-C, hypertriglyceridemia, and elevated Lp(a) — broader than the 2018 version

## Pages 11–20: Abbreviations, Evaluation/Diagnosis, Screening, Lipid Measurement, ApoB, Lp(a), Monitoring, Lipoprotein Goals, Lifestyle Management

### Screening (Section 3.1)
- Universal lipid screening starting at age 19 in adults (every 5 years) — COR 1
- Children screened ages 9–11 — COR 1
- Cascade screening for FH families starting at age 2 — COR 2a
- Child screening justified by evidence that subclinical atherosclerosis on carotid imaging can appear as early as age 8–10

### LDL-C Measurement (Section 3.2)
- **Martin/Hopkins and Sampson/NIH equations now preferred** over the Friedewald equation
- Friedewald acknowledged as unreliable at TG ≥400 mg/dL
- Advanced lipoprotein testing (NMR, gradient gel, etc.) — **Class 3 "No Benefit"** for routine risk assessment

### ApoB Measurement (Section 3.3)
- Positioned as a better predictor of ASCVD than LDL-C, particularly in CKM syndrome, diabetes, or elevated TG
- LDL-C can be misleadingly "at goal" while atherogenic particle number (apoB) remains high
- **Critical observation:** This implicitly acknowledges that LDL-C alone is an incomplete picture of atherogenic risk

### Lp(a) Measurement (Section 3.4)
- **Universal measurement at least once in all adults — COR 1** (major upgrade)
- Table 4 shows graded risk: 1.4-fold at 125 nmol/L (50 mg/dL), 2-fold at 250 nmol/L, ~4-fold at 430 nmol/L
- >90% genetically determined — current therapies can't meaningfully lower it
- Actionable step: more aggressive management of other risk factors when Lp(a) is elevated

### Figure 1 — Lipoprotein Goals for ASCVD Risk Reduction
Key clinical reference chart. Targets include:
- LDL-C <55 mg/dL for very high-risk ASCVD and CAC ≥1000
- LDL-C <70 mg/dL for ASCVD not at very high risk
- LDL-C <100 mg/dL for subclinical atherosclerosis and diabetes with risk factors
- Subclinical atherosclerosis section using CAC scores is much more granular than the ESC approach

### Lifestyle Management (Section 4.1)
- Primordial prevention starting in childhood — COR 1 (Level A)
- Dietary patterns: Mediterranean, DASH, vegetarian have the strongest evidence for LDL-C reduction and CV outcomes
- **Hypertriglyceridemia dietary guidance (Figure 2)**: Practical and well-supported; low-carb diets acknowledged as effectively lowering TG
- Carbohydrate-induced hypertriglyceridemia is explicitly recognized — a partial nod toward the metabolic health perspective

## Pages 21–30: Weight Management, Physical Activity, Dietary Supplements, RDN Referral, Pharmacological Therapy Overview, Primary Prevention Framework

### Weight Management (Section 4.1.3)
- Acknowledges non-HDL-C and apoB better reflect atherogenic risk than LDL-C in obesity
- Ketogenic diets: acknowledged to reduce TG and produce short-term weight loss, but can substantially increase LDL-C in some individuals (particularly with high animal product consumption)

### Dietary Supplements (Section 4.1.5)
- **Class 3 "No Benefit"** — matching ESC/EAS position exactly
- SPORT trial cited: rosuvastatin 5 mg reduced LDL-C ~38%; none of the supplements (fish oil, cinnamon, garlic, turmeric, plant sterols, red yeast rice) produced a statistically significant decrease vs placebo

### Pharmacological Therapy (Section 4.2)
- **Table 5** — comprehensive drug reference covering statins, ezetimibe, PCSK9 mAbs, bempedoic acid, inclisiran (CVOT still in progress), bile acid sequestrants, lomitapide, evinacumab, fibrates, icosapent ethyl, niacin (essentially discouraged), and olezarsen
- **Table 6 — Statin Intensity**: High-intensity (≥50% LDL-C reduction) = atorvastatin 40–80 mg or rosuvastatin 20–40 mg (unchanged from 2018)
- Inclisiran still lacks CV outcomes data — guideline includes it but flags the evidence gap
- Combination therapy approach is now much more explicit
- Bempedoic acid gets significant coverage for statin-intolerant patients

### Primary Prevention — PREVENT-ASCVD (Section 4.2.3)
- Replaces old Pooled Cohort Equations, which overpredicted risk by 40–50%
- New risk categories: low (<3%), borderline (3% to <5%), intermediate (5% to <10%), high (≥10%)
- "Individualized benefit-risk discussion" heavily emphasized — genuine improvement toward shared decision-making
- **Key admission (page 29):** "Once atherosclerosis has developed, risk may remain above that of individuals without atherosclerosis even with intensive LDL-C-lowering therapy" — a quiet concession that the damage mechanism isn't purely lipid-driven

## Pages 31–40: PREVENT-ASCVD Details, Risk Thresholds, Risk Enhancers, CAC Scoring, Primary Prevention Algorithms

### PREVENT-ASCVD Equations (Table 11)
- Derived from ~3.3 million contemporary US adults
- Covers ages 30–79, sex-specific
- **Race/ethnicity is NOT a variable** (unlike old PCE)
- Optional inputs: HbA1c, eGFR, albumin/creatinine ratio, zip code (social deprivation proxy)
- Predicts both 10-year and 30-year risk

### Table 12 — PCE to PREVENT Crosswalk
- Old PCE ≥5% maps to PREVENT-ASCVD ≥3%
- Lowered threshold for considering statin therapy, but only because old calculator overpredicted

### Figure 4 — NNT vs NND Analysis
- At ≥3% 10-year PREVENT-ASCVD risk: NNT with moderate-intensity statin to prevent 1 ASCVD event ≈ 100; NND to cause 1 case of diabetes ≈ 100
- **At the threshold where they recommend considering treatment, preventing one event roughly equals causing one case of diabetes** — a thin margin of benefit

### Risk Enhancers (Table 13 & Section 4.2.3.3)
- Include: chronic inflammatory diseases (lupus, RA, psoriasis), hsCRP ≥2 mg/L, CKM syndrome, Lp(a) ≥125 nmol/L, reproductive risk markers, family history, South Asian ancestry
- **Inclusion of inflammation markers and autoimmune diseases as independent risk enhancers implicitly acknowledges that atherosclerosis has major inflammatory/immune-mediated components beyond lipid deposition**
- JUPITER trial: rosuvastatin benefit correlated with hsCRP reduction

### CAC Scoring (Section 4.2.3.6) — Most Granular CAC Guidance in Any Guideline
- CAC = 0: strongly associated with low 10-year ASCVD event rates; reasonable to defer statin therapy
- ~40% of intermediate-risk adults have CAC = 0
- CAC ≥300: risk comparable to established ASCVD
- CAC ≥1000: treated like secondary prevention
- AI-based algorithms for detecting incidental CAC on noncardiac CT scans acknowledged (COR 1)
- Repeat CAC testing every 3–7 years when deferred
- **CAC measures the disease directly rather than inferring risk from proxy biomarkers — the fact that CAC = 0 can "de-risk" someone despite elevated LDL-C further supports the argument that LDL-C alone doesn't tell the whole story**

### Primary Prevention Algorithm (Section 4.2.3.7 & Figure 6)
- Detailed recommendations stratified by risk level
- At low risk (<3%): adults with LDL-C <160 and 30-year risk <10% get lifestyle counseling only
- **Class 3 "No Benefit"** for treating people with baseline untreated LDL-C <70 and non-HDL-C <100 without additional risk factors

## Pages 41–50: Primary Prevention Supportive Text, Severe Hypercholesterolemia, FH, HoFH

### Critical Admissions in Primary Prevention Text
- **No RCTs of LLT initiation exist in individuals at <3% 10-year risk** — recommendation for young/low-risk individuals based on inference, not direct trial evidence
- CTT meta-analysis: in low-risk group, NNT ≈ 100 to prevent 1 event, which **equals the NND to cause 1 case of diabetes** — framed as "net benefit" but essentially a coin flip
- Legitimate concerns about **polypharmacy in limited life expectancy** acknowledged; reasonable to discontinue statins when life expectancy <1 year
- **"There is no clear net benefit to initiation of LLT for individuals with untreated LDL-C levels <70 mg/dL and non-HDL-C levels <100 mg/dL"** in primary prevention

### Table 15 — Secondary Causes of Elevated LDL-C
- Lists dietary, metabolic (hypothyroidism, nephrotic syndrome, diabetes/insulin resistance, obesity), drug, and physiological causes
- **Guideline explicitly requires ruling out secondary causes before assuming primary hypercholesterolemia — implicitly acknowledges that elevated LDL-C is often a symptom of underlying metabolic dysfunction rather than a primary cause**
- Ketogenic diets can cause severe hypercholesterolemia phenotype; dietary history required before escalating pharmacotherapy

### FH (Sections 4.2.4.1–4.2.4.3)
- Standard risk calculators **should not be used** in FH (Class 3: Harm — they underestimate risk)
- CAC = 0 **should not** justify deferring statin therapy in FH
- Panel-based genetic testing (LDLR, APOB, PCSK9) now COR 1 for confirmed/probable FH
- Only 54% of people with FH-causing variants have LDL-C ≥190 mg/dL — a rigid cutoff misses nearly half

### HoFH (Section 4.2.4.4 & Figure 8)
- Most aggressive treatment algorithm in the document
- Statin → ezetimibe/PCSK9 mAb/bempedoic acid → evinacumab → lomitapide → lipoprotein apheresis
- Median first MACE at age 37 (high-income countries), 25 (lower-income)
- Lipid specialist referral mandatory (COR 1)

## Pages 51–60: Diabetes, Secondary ASCVD Prevention, Subclinical Coronary Atherosclerosis

### Diabetes Without Established ASCVD (Section 4.2.5)
- Ages 40–75: Moderate-intensity statin universal for diabetics regardless of calculated risk — COR 1 (Level A)
- Diabetes-specific risk enhancers (Table 17): long duration (≥10y T2D, ≥20y T1D), albuminuria, low eGFR, retinopathy, neuropathy, ABI <0.9
- Ages 20–39 with diabetes: only COR 2b — **limited information and no evidence on statin benefit** in this age group
- Ages >75 with diabetes: COR 2b after clinician-patient discussion
- IPE mentioned for residual risk with elevated TG on statin therapy
- Figure 9 includes optional apoB goal <90 mg/dL — first time in a diabetes pathway

### Secondary ASCVD Prevention (Section 4.2.6)
- **Not very high risk ASCVD**: High-intensity statin → LDL-C <70, non-HDL-C <100; add ezetimibe/PCSK9 mAb/bempedoic acid if not at goal (COR 2a); optional goal <55/<85
- **Very high risk ASCVD**: High-intensity statin → LDL-C <55, non-HDL-C <85, optional apoB <55; ezetimibe and/or PCSK9 mAb COR 1 (Level A)
- **ESC comparison**: ESC targets <55 mg/dL for all very high-risk as Class I; ACC/AHA uses a more layered approach with <55 as optional for not-very-high-risk
- Evolocumab reduced ASCVD risk **even at baseline LDL-C <20 mg/dL** — raises question of whether mechanism is purely lipid-driven or partly anti-inflammatory
- Cost: evolocumab/alirocumab ~$5,850/year; cost-effectiveness improves at LDL-C >100 mg/dL
- HFrEF due to ischemic ASCVD: COR 2b — CORONA and GISSI-HF both failed primary endpoints
- Inclisiran: LDL-C reduction ~50%, but CVOTs pending (ORION 4 expected 2026, VICTORION-2 expected 2027)

### Subclinical Coronary Atherosclerosis (Section 4.2.7)
- CAC ≥1000: LDL-C <55, non-HDL-C <85 (same as very high-risk ASCVD)
- CAC 300–999: LDL-C <70, non-HDL-C <100
- CAC 100–299 or ≥75th percentile: ≥50% reduction, LDL-C <70, non-HDL-C <100
- CAC 1–99 and <75th percentile: moderate-intensity statin, LDL-C <100
- Incidental CAC on noncardiac CT (AI-detected): COR 2a for initiating statin therapy
- **"Nearly linear relationship between CAC scores and MACE"** — directly measuring disease is more informative than lipid proxies

## Pages 61–70: Children, Young Adults, Older Adults, Pregnancy, Ancestry, Heart Failure, Chronic Inflammatory Diseases, CKD

### Children and Adolescents (Section 4.2.8.1)
- Table 18: childhood lipid thresholds (LDL-C abnormal ≥130 mg/dL)
- Table 19: FDA-approved pediatric lipid-lowering agents (statins from age 8–10, ezetimibe from 10, evolocumab from 10, alirocumab from 8)
- **No studies have sufficient follow-up to report ASCVD event reduction in children** — treatment based on assumption that lowering childhood exposure reduces adult events
- Only 1 trial (pravastatin in HeFH youth) showed lower ASCVD events over 20 years vs parental controls

### Young Adults 18–39 (Section 4.2.8.2)
- COR 1 for lifestyle optimization
- **RCTs for LLT in young adults ages 18–29 demonstrating ASCVD risk reduction have not been performed** — pharmacotherapy described as "a matter of clinical judgment and patient preference in the absence of available evidence"

### Older Adults (Section 4.2.8.3)
- RCT data for >75 is **limited**; relative risk reductions lower than younger populations; **no evidence of overall mortality benefit**
- CAC = 0 in older adults: LLT benefits **unlikely to outweigh potential risks**
- Life expectancy <1 year: reasonable to **discontinue** LLT (RCT showed improved QOL without increased short-term mortality)
- NNT for primary prevention ages 50–75: 100 adults treated for 2.5 years to prevent 1 MACE; similar data for ≥75 is lacking
- Two ongoing RCTs in adults >70 may clarify

### Pregnancy (Section 4.2.8.4)
- Table 20: comprehensive drug safety reference for pregnancy/lactation
- Statins: discontinue 1–2 months before conception (COR 1); bile acid sequestrants only drugs considered safe
- Lipoprotein apheresis reasonable for HoFH during pregnancy (COR 2a)
- FDA shifted in 2021 from blanket contraindication to individualized risk-benefit for statins in pregnancy
- Pravastatin in 173 pregnant women significantly reduced preeclampsia risk (13.8% vs 26.7%)

### Ancestry Considerations (Section 4.2.8.5)
- Table 21: South Asian ancestry = high-risk group; increased diabetes at lower BMI in Asian populations; higher Lp(a) in non-Hispanic Black individuals; statin sensitivity in East Asian ancestry
- **Race/ethnicity stated as social constructs**; PREVENT-ASCVD deliberately excludes race
- Focus on modifiable factors and social determinants of health

### Heart Failure (Section 4.2.8.6)
- **Class 3: No Benefit** for initiating LLT in HFrEF without clinical ASCVD
- CORONA, GISSI-HF, PEARL all failed to show benefit
- **Lowering LDL-C doesn't help HF patients without ischemic disease — undermines universal causality argument**

### Chronic Inflammatory Diseases (Section 4.2.8.7)
- RA, lupus, psoriasis, IBD independently increase ASCVD risk through **LDL-C-independent pathways (inflammation)**
- Standard risk calculators underestimate risk in these populations
- Statins may have "modest direct anti-inflammatory effects" but RCT evidence specifically in CID populations is lacking
- Anti-inflammatory therapies can have both lipid and nonlipid cardiovascular effects
- **This section directly supports the view that atherosclerosis has major inflammatory/immune-mediated components beyond lipid deposition**

### CKD Stage 3+ (Section 4.2.8.8)
- COR 1 for moderate- or high-intensity statin with ezetimibe in CKD stage 3+ (ages 40–75)
- Clinical ASCVD + CKD: LDL-C <55, non-HDL-C <85
- Hemodialysis: weaker COR 2b — benefit less clear in ESRD
- Statins safe in CKD with or without ASCVD; bempedoic acid not evaluated in severe CKD/dialysis
- Inclisiran pooled analysis (ORION -9, -10, -11): similar LDL-C lowering in mild/moderate CKD
- Dialysis patients — initiating statin not beneficial in 2 large RCTs; continuing LLT if already on it is reasonable

## Pages 71–72: HIV (Section 4.2.8.9)

- **COR 1 / LOE B-R:** Statin therapy recommended for PLHIV aged 40–75 on stable combination ART to reduce ASCVD risk and coronary atherosclerosis progression
- **REPRIEVE trial:** Pitavastatin 4 mg daily in 7769 PLHIV over median 5.1 years → 35% relative reduction in first MACE compared with placebo (95% CI, 0.48–0.90; P=0.002)
- Pitavastatin selected specifically because it avoids CYP3A4 metabolism → minimal DDI with antiretrovirals
- PLHIV have increased ASCVD risk independent of traditional risk factors, due to insulin resistance, atherogenic dyslipidemia, partial lipodystrophy, and abnormal innate/adaptive immunity → accelerated atherosclerosis
- REPRIEVE substudy: pitavastatin reduced noncalcified plaque progression on CCTA
- Median PCE-determined 10-year risk in REPRIEVE was 4.5%; median LDL-C was 108 mg/dL — benefit seen even at low-to-moderate baseline risk
- **Table 22:** Comprehensive statin–antiretroviral DDI reference covering protease inhibitors, NNRTIs, NRTIs, and integrase strand transfer inhibitors with specific dosing recommendations for each statin
- For PLHIV <40, guidance defers to standard primary prevention sections

## Pages 72–74: Cancer (Section 4.2.8.10)

- **COR 1 / B-NR:** Adult cancer survivors with life expectancy ≥2 years who otherwise qualify for LLT should be treated similarly to people without cancer history
- **COR 1 / B-NR:** Adults with active cancer on statin should continue treatment unless specific DDI concern or life expectancy <1 year
- **COR 2b / B-R:** In adults with active cancer, statin initiation may be considered to prevent anthracycline-induced cardiotoxicity
- ASCVD risk elevated among cancer survivors: standardized mortality ratio of fatal heart disease 2.24 (95% CI, 2.23–2.25)
- Among 5-year cancer survivors at low oncologic risk, heart disease death risk exceeds cancer death risk
- Statins do not increase cancer recurrence (pooled analysis of PCSK9 mAb trials also showed no signal)
- Cancer survivors are paradoxically more likely to discontinue statin therapy than the general population despite their increased risk
- Statin discontinuation in older adults with cancer associated with higher HF hospitalization risk (HR 1.24)
- Statins may enhance activity of some anticancer treatments and provide protection against chemotherapy-induced cardiomyopathy

## Pages 74–80: Hypertriglyceridemia (Section 4.2.9)

### Recommendations (7 total, COR 1 through 2b)
1. **COR 1 / B-NR:** Lifestyle management first-line for persistently elevated TG ≥150 mg/dL — diet (low added sugars, reduced alcohol and saturated fat), exercise, and weight loss (5–10%) if overweight/obese
2. **COR 1 / B-R:** In adults with clinical ASCVD on maximally tolerated statin with persistently elevated TG 150–999 mg/dL, intensification of LDL-C-lowering therapy is recommended to reduce ASCVD risk
3. **COR 1 / B-R:** In FCS with TG ≥1000 mg/dL, **olezarsen** is recommended as adjunct to diet to lower TG and reduce pancreatitis risk
4. **COR 2b / B-R:** In adults ≥50 with ASCVD or diabetes + ≥1 ASCVD risk factor, persistently elevated TG 150–499 mg/dL and LDL-C <100 mg/dL on maximally tolerated statin, **icosapent ethyl (IPE)** may be reasonable to lower ASCVD risk — **notably weaker recommendation than ESC's Class IIa**
5. **COR 1 / A:** In adults aged 40–75 with ASCVD or diabetes and persistent hypertriglyceridemia (≥150–499 mg/dL), use PREVENT-ASCVD equations to guide benefit-risk discussion
6. **COR 2a / B-NR:** For severe hypertriglyceridemia (TG ≥500 mg/dL, especially ≥1000), fibric acid derivatives or prescription omega-3 fatty acids are reasonable to lower TG and reduce pancreatitis risk
7. **COR 2a / B-NR:** In adults with hypertriglyceridemia (TG ≥150 mg/dL), measurement of non-HDL-C or apoB preferred over LDL-C to guide clinical decision-making

### Key Supporting Evidence and Commentary
- Persistent hypertriglyceridemia defined as fasting TG ≥150 mg/dL (1.7 mmol/L) after evaluation and management of secondary causes and ≥4–12 weeks of lifestyle management
- CV risk from hypertriglyceridemia is highest at TG 500–880 mg/dL; those >880 mg/dL — initial priority is pancreatitis risk reduction
- **Only purified EPA trials showed CV benefit** — combined EPA/DHA (STRENGTH trial) did not; recommendation explicitly specifies this distinction
- REDUCE-IT (IPE): 25% relative reduction in primary composite endpoint, but higher rates of new-onset atrial fibrillation and serious bleeding in IPE group
- REDUCE-IT biomarker subanalysis: no significant improvement in atherogenic lipids, lipoproteins, or inflammatory markers in IPE group vs placebo; placebo (mineral oil) may have caused deleterious changes that exaggerated the treatment effect
- Fibric acid derivatives lower TG by 30–50% but do not provide incremental ASCVD risk reduction when added to statin; first-line for TG <1000 remains statin
- Gemfibrozil not recommended; preference for fenofibrate due to fewer DDI and lower myopathy risk in combination with statins
- **Olezarsen** (apoC-III ASO): FDA-approved for FCS; phase 3 demonstrated −43.5% TG reduction vs placebo (95% CI, −52.2 to −17.9; P<0.001) with fewer pancreatitis episodes
- DHA-containing omega-3 products can raise LDL-C (but not apoB)
- **Figures 14–17:** Four separate hypertriglyceridemia management flowcharts by clinical scenario (ASCVD + HTG; diabetes without ASCVD + HTG; no ASCVD/diabetes + HTG; severe HTG ≥500 mg/dL)
- In hypertriglyceridemia with discordance between apoB/LDL-C/non-HDL-C, apoB and non-HDL-C are more accurate estimators of ASCVD risk than LDL-C

## Pages 81–82: Approach to Patients With Elevated Lp(a) (Section 4.2.10)

### Recommendations (2 total)
1. **COR 1 / B-NR:** In all individuals with elevated Lp(a) ≥125 nmol/L (≥50 mg/dL), optimal early control of modifiable CV risk factors is recommended to reduce ASCVD risk
2. **COR 1 / B-R:** In individuals with clinical ASCVD and elevated Lp(a) who have not achieved LDL-C and non-HDL-C treatment goals on maximally tolerated statin therapy, the addition of a PCSK9 mAb with proven CV benefit is recommended to achieve treatment goals and reduce ASCVD risk

### Key Supporting Evidence and Commentary
- Lp(a) is ~90% genetically determined; lifestyle management minimally impacts it
- Relative risk from Lp(a) is multiplicative with other risk factors
- ~20% of the population has elevated Lp(a) ≥125 nmol/L (50 mg/dL), corresponding to ~40% higher relative risk vs population median
- Lp(a) of ~200–250 nmol/L (~80–100 mg/dL) doubles risk; ~430 nmol/L (~180 mg/dL) increases risk ~4-fold — equivalent to HeFH
- **Statins do not lower Lp(a)** — may modestly increase it; this is not a reason to discontinue given the strong overall CV benefit
- PCSK9 mAbs and lipoprotein apheresis lower Lp(a) as well as LDL-C
- PCSK9 mAbs are not currently FDA-approved specifically for Lp(a) lowering; however, post hoc analyses of FOURIER and ODYSSEY Outcomes suggest patients with higher Lp(a) derive greater benefit
- In a subgroup analysis of 10 ODYSSEY phase 3 trials (alirocumab vs control): the association between Lp(a) reduction and CV events was seen only in patients with Lp(a) ≥50 mg/dL (125 nmol/L; P for interaction=0.05)
- **Lipoprotein apheresis** is FDA-approved for patients with Lp(a) ≥60 mg/dL (≥130 nmol/L) who also have FH and CAD or PAD (Table 16)
- Post hoc from 2 primary prevention trials: aspirin reduced CV events in individuals with high Lp(a), but prospective trials are needed
- **Specific Lp(a)-lowering therapies** (mRNA therapies, oral small-molecule inhibitors targeting Lp(a) production) are being investigated in randomized clinical outcomes trials — none yet approved
- AHA's Life's Essential 8 lifestyle changes associated with 67% lower ASCVD risk among those with elevated Lp(a)

## Pages 82–85: Statin-Attributed Muscle Symptoms (Section 4.2.11)

### Recommendations (8 total — most granular SAMS framework in any guideline)
1. **COR 1 / C-LD:** In adults with SAMS, evaluate for secondary causes (Table 24), perform objective clinical measures of muscle strength, and measure CK to assess severity
2. **COR 1 / B-R:** Acknowledge patient concerns, inform the patient of heightened ASCVD risk associated with statin discontinuation, and provide alternative treatment options
3. **COR 1 / B-R:** In adults with clinical ASCVD and SAMS unable to achieve recommended goals: use reduced statin dose (if tolerable) plus bempedoic acid, ezetimibe, or PCSK9 mAb alone or in combination
4. **COR 1 / B-R:** In adults without clinical ASCVD at high ASCVD risk (PREVENT ≥10%, or CAC ≥300 AU, or women >65 / men >60 with diabetes): add bempedoic acid and/or ezetimibe to lower LDL-C to <70 mg/dL and non-HDL-C to <100 mg/dL
5. **COR 1 / B-R:** In adults without clinical ASCVD at high ASCVD risk (PREVENT ≥10%, or CAC ≥300 AU, or diabetes) unable to achieve goals: PCSK9 mAb recommended
6. **COR 2a / B-NR:** In adults with clinical ASCVD and SAMS unable to achieve goals on bempedoic acid ± ezetimibe, and unable to tolerate/obtain PCSK9 mAb or who prefer less frequent dosing: **inclisiran** is reasonable (CV outcomes pending)
7. **COR 2b / B-R:** In adults without clinical ASCVD with SAMS at borderline-to-intermediate ASCVD risk (PREVENT 3% to <10%) and uncertain ezetimibe/bempedoic acid benefit: CAC scoring may be reasonable to inform decision-making
8. **COR 2b / B-NR:** In adults without clinical ASCVD with SAMS at borderline-to-intermediate risk (PREVENT 3% to <10%): ezetimibe and/or bempedoic acid may be reasonable to lower LDL-C to <100 mg/dL

### Key Supporting Evidence and Commentary
- **"Drucebo effect" explicitly acknowledged** — many SAMS cases driven by patient expectations rather than the statin itself; RCT incidence of muscle symptoms is low (~1–5% above placebo), while observational/real-world incidence is considerably higher (~10–15%)
- Before attributing symptoms to statins: exclude excessive muscular activity, DDI, primary muscle/metabolic disorders, hypothyroidism
- Statin-related etiology supported by: new-onset bilateral symmetrical proximal muscle pain/weakness occurring within weeks of initiation or dose increase, resolving on discontinuation, recurring on rechallenge
- Most patients can be successfully managed on a well-tolerated regimen (reduced dose, alternate-day dosing, switch statin, or nonstatin combination)
- Rhabdomyolysis is rare; statin-associated autoimmune myopathy (anti-HMGCR antibodies, persistent CK elevation, incomplete resolution on discontinuation) is very rare
- **CoQ10 supplementation: Class 3 "No Benefit"** — despite some evidence of a small benefit, the preponderance of evidence does not support routine use
- **Routine CK measurement: Class 3 "No Benefit"** — CK levels not useful unless severe symptoms (measure only if severe myalgias or muscle weakness and CK ≥10× ULN)
- **Table 24:** 14 characteristics associated with increased SAMS risk: age ≥65, low BMI, female sex, obesity, hypothyroidism, diabetes, CKD, chronic liver disease, alcohol use, vigorous exercise, high-dose statin, diseases associated with myalgia, pharmacotherapy affecting statin metabolism, and gene variants affecting statin metabolism (SLC01B1)
- **Figure 18:** Comprehensive SAMS management flowchart — tiers patients by clinical ASCVD status and PREVENT-ASCVD risk level, with specific LDL-C goals and escalation pathways at each tier; includes inclisiran as COR 2a for ASCVD patients unable to access PCSK9 mAb
- CAC = 0 in patients with SAMS, without ASCVD, particularly men ≥40 / women ≥50 without diabetes or smoking: may support deferring LLT for up to 5 years

## Pages 85–88: Complications of Management (Section 5)

### 5.1 — Medication Safety and Therapy-Associated Side Effects

#### Recommendations
1. **COR 1 / A:** Individualized clinician-patient discussion prior to initiating any LLT — review benefits, risks, promote patient engagement and medication adherence
2. **COR 1 / A:** In adults with elevated diabetes risk or new-onset diabetes, **continue statin therapy** with added emphasis on adherence, net clinical benefit, and lifestyle management — CV benefit outweighs diabetes risk
3. **COR 2a / B-R:** In adults at elevated ASCVD risk with chronic stable liver disease (including MASLD), it is reasonable to treat with statin therapy to reduce ASCVD risk
4. **Class 3: No Benefit / B-R:** Routine CoQ10 supplementation is not recommended to treat or prevent SAMS
5. **Class 3: No Benefit / A:** Routine CK measurement in asymptomatic adults on statin therapy is not useful
6. **Class 3: No Benefit / B-NR:** Routine hepatic function measurement in asymptomatic adults on statin therapy (without severe hepatotoxicity symptoms) is not useful

#### Key Supporting Evidence and Commentary
- **Table 25:** Comprehensive safety reference for all LDL-C-lowering medication classes — statins, ezetimibe, PCSK9 mAbs (evolocumab/alirocumab), bempedoic acid, inclisiran, bile acid sequestrants, lomitapide, evinacumab; includes common side effects, contraindications, and monitoring notes
- **Table 26:** Safety considerations for triglyceride-lowering medications — fibrates, omega-3 fatty acids (IPE, omega-3-acid ethyl esters), niacin, olezarsen
- Niacin: essentially discouraged due to poor tolerability, lack of proven CV outcomes benefit, and multiple side effects (flushing, GI symptoms, hepatotoxicity, insulin resistance); infrequently used
- Gemfibrozil: avoid combination with any statin; fenofibrate or fenofibric acid can be combined with statin with monitoring
- DHA-containing omega-3 products: can raise LDL-C but not apoB; new-onset atrial fibrillation/flutter reported; possible increased bleeding when combined with antithrombotic agents
- **Statins and diabetes:** Statins slightly increase risk of progression to new-onset diabetes in those with predisposing risk factors (BMI ≥30, fasting glucose ≥100, metabolic syndrome, HbA1c 6.0–6.4%), especially with higher-intensity statin use; meta-analysis showed a small, clinically insignificant increase in HbA1c of 0.06–0.08%; **potential new-onset diabetes is not a contraindication to statin therapy or indication for discontinuation** — the CV benefit in these patients outweighs the diabetes risk
- **Statins and liver disease:** Not contraindicated in active liver disease, chronic stable liver disease, or MASLD; safety data reaffirm no increased risk of hepatotoxicity or hepatic failure; asymptomatic transaminase elevations are common, generally within first 3 months, return to baseline in ~70% with continued use; termed "adaptations" rather than "injury"; serious hepatotoxicity ~1 in 100,000 treated individuals; routine hepatic transaminase monitoring discontinued by FDA recommendation in 2012
- **SAMS diagnosis is challenging:** No definitive diagnostic criteria exist; multiple potential mechanisms (CoQ10 depletion, altered statin metabolism, excessive muscular activity, patient expectations); in the great majority of patients, a well-tolerated regimen can be devised
- **Statin-associated autoimmune myopathy:** Rare but serious; marked by anti-HMGCR antibodies, persistent CK elevation, and incomplete resolution on statin discontinuation; requires statin cessation and avoidance of all potential myotoxic pharmacotherapy, with possible additional immunotherapy

### 5.2 — Statin-Cardiovascular Drug Interactions

#### Recommendations
1. **COR 1 / B-NR:** In adults for whom the decision is made to initiate statin therapy, review concomitant medications and assess for potential DDI with other cardiovascular medications to minimize risk of adverse events

#### Key Supporting Evidence and Commentary
- Most statin-related DDIs are mediated through the CYP450 enzyme system (primarily CYP3A4 and CYP2C9), P-glycoprotein, and organic anion-transporting polypeptide
- **CYP3A4-metabolized statins** (lovastatin, simvastatin, and to a lesser extent atorvastatin) pose the highest DDI risk
- **CYP2C9-metabolized statins** (fluvastatin, pitavastatin, rosuvastatin) have significant CYP2C9 involvement
- **Pravastatin** does not undergo CYP450 metabolism — fewest DDIs
- **Table 27:** Specific dosing recommendations for DDI between statins and 11 commonly coprescribed cardiovascular drugs: amiodarone, amlodipine, bempedoic acid, colchicine, conivaptan, diltiazem, dronedarone, gemfibrozil, lomitapide, ranolazine, ticagrelor, and verapamil
- Gemfibrozil: avoid combination with all statins (particularly atorvastatin, lovastatin, pitavastatin, pravastatin, rosuvastatin, simvastatin)
- ~66% of statin DDI information in prescribing labels is supported by dedicated clinical DDI studies; 34% is extrapolated from other statins
- AHA scientific statement emphasizes the importance of evaluating statin DDI, particularly given widespread cardiovascular polypharmacy

## Pages 89–90: Evidence Gaps and Future Directions (Section 6)

### 6.1 — Limitations and Knowledge Gaps
Nine key unanswered questions identified:
1. What innovative approaches can better engage patients, community members, health systems, and clinicians in implementing guidelines, improving adherence, and addressing therapeutic inertia?
2. Can LDL-C-lowering therapy be safely initiated and managed by pharmacists as part of the care team?
3. **Does early LLT in low- or borderline-risk younger individuals with subclinical atherosclerosis or elevated atherogenic lipoproteins reduce long-term ASCVD risk?**
4. **How valid are serial imaging measures of plaque (CCTA-based, intravascular ultrasound) as surrogate measures of ASCVD risk reduction compared with event-driven trials?**
5. Should CCTA be used for screening in higher-risk younger individuals with CAC = 0?
6. **Does pharmacological lowering of Lp(a) reduce ASCVD risk? What are optimal treatment thresholds across different populations?**
7. **How can clinical trials better incorporate apoB alongside or instead of LDL-C to guide future treatment recommendations?**
8. How should the polygenic risk score be incorporated into risk assessment and treatment selection?
9. **Can we develop validated and effective scalable shared decision-making models for personalized clinician-patient risk discussion?**

Additional gaps identified: research needed on reproductive-age risk markers, mechanisms of ASCVD risk in relation to conventional risk factors, social determinants of health and psychosocial stressors, racial/ethnic disparities in ASCVD events, and lipid changes with gender-affirming hormone therapy in transgender individuals.

### 6.2 — Randomized Controlled Trials
- ACC/AHA guidelines are based largely on outcomes from randomized CVOTs spanning 70 years
- Greater LDL-C reductions produce greater ASCVD risk reductions — established across both statin and nonstatin therapies
- Nonstatin therapies (ezetimibe, PCSK9 mAbs, bempedoic acid) now have demonstrated efficacy and safety in CVOTs
- Key unresolved questions: Can LLT be safely pharmacist-initiated? Does early institution of LLT in low/borderline-risk young adults with subclinical atherosclerosis reduce long-term events? How valid are serial plaque imaging surrogate endpoints? Does pharmacological Lp(a) lowering reduce events? How should apoB guide treatment in future trials? How should polygenic risk scores inform treatment? Can shared decision-making models be validated at scale?

### 6.3 — Improving Cardiovascular Risk Assessment
- PREVENT equations are an improvement over PCE but individual-level prediction still has inherent limitations — applying population-level probabilities to individual patients is imprecise
- Local recalibration of risk-prediction models to specific populations could improve accuracy but should not be routine due to potential for systematic misclassification at guideline decision thresholds
- More frequent use of reclassification tools (CAC scoring, other imaging) recommended in the population of interest
- **Dynamic risk models** (eg, the Million Hearts Model, which accounts for baseline risk and subsequent response to treatment) have shown potential to reduce ASCVD events and are a promising future direction
- Better strategies for communicating risk and potential benefit to patients in preventive decision-making are needed

### 6.4 — Refining the Clinician-Patient Risk Discussion
- Shared decision-making described as **"the cornerstone"** of long-term partnership between clinician and patient for improving outcomes
- LDL-C-lowering therapy is meant to be a long-term or lifetime commitment — individual communication and engagement are as important as the pharmacotherapy itself
- Decision-making is increasingly complex given the expanded range of nonstatin therapies, multiple treatment goals (LDL-C, non-HDL-C, apoB), and variable patient preferences regarding injection therapies, cost, and pill burden
- Studies on optimal methods for clinician-patient interaction in dyslipidemia management are vital
- Individualized benefit-risk framing is essential — future encounters should address treatment response, adherence, medication-associated symptoms, and reaffirm benefit

## Running Themes and Critical Observations

### Strengths of This Guideline
1. **Broader scope** — moving beyond LDL-C to include apoB, Lp(a), and TG is a genuine improvement
2. **CAC scoring** — treating CAC as a direct disease measure is the most clinically useful innovation; CAC = 0 as a de-risking tool is powerful
3. **Shared decision-making** — the CPR framework and benefit-risk discussion emphasis is a real improvement
4. **Honest evidence gaps** — multiple sections acknowledge where RCT data doesn't exist (young adults, children, primary prevention at low risk, older adults, Lp(a) lowering)
5. **Treatment goals restored** — specific LDL-C/non-HDL-C/apoB targets are clinically actionable
6. **PREVENT-ASCVD equations** — better calibrated, race-neutral, includes 30-year risk
7. **SAMS management** — the most comprehensive framework in any guideline; acknowledges the drucebo effect; provides specific escalation pathways tiered by risk
8. **Hypertriglyceridemia** — tiered, practical, with separate algorithms by clinical scenario; honest about the REDUCE-IT mineral oil controversy
9. **Special populations** — HIV (REPRIEVE), cancer survivors, pregnancy, CKD, ancestry — all receive dedicated sections with tailored guidance

### Weaknesses and Tensions
1. **Cumulative exposure model is inferential** — no RCTs in young/low-risk adults; recommendation based on Mendelian randomization and observational data
2. **NNT/NND parity at threshold** — at the ≥3% risk threshold for considering treatment, preventing 1 ASCVD event requires treating ~100 people, and the same number would cause 1 case of diabetes
3. **People with high LDL and no disease** — the guideline doesn't adequately address why many people with lifetime high LDL-C have clean arteries (CAC = 0 prevalence in intermediate-risk adults is ~40%)
4. **Inflammation acknowledged but not centered** — CID section, hsCRP as risk enhancer, and JUPITER trial all point to inflammation as a major independent driver, yet the framework still treats lipids as primary
5. **HF non-benefit** — the Class 3 rating for LLT in non-ischemic HF is a significant concession that lowering LDL-C isn't universally beneficial
6. **Residual risk** — the admission that risk remains elevated even after intensive LDL-C lowering suggests other pathological processes (inflammation, endothelial dysfunction) are at work
7. **Inclisiran without outcomes data** — included in multiple pathways (SAMS management, secondary prevention) despite pending CVOTs; premature recommendation
8. **IPE controversy acknowledged but not resolved** — REDUCE-IT mineral oil placebo concern is noted but IPE still gets a COR 2b; the guideline explicitly notes that the biomarker subanalysis showed no improvement in atherogenic lipids or inflammatory markers in the IPE group
9. **Lp(a) — no approved specific therapies** — universal measurement is COR 1, but the only actionable step is intensifying other risk factor management; specific Lp(a)-lowering agents are still in trials
